Overview
This research blueprint proposes a fully local generative architecture for real-time speech and avatar animation that goes beyond traditional transformer or diffusion pipelines. The system uses a spectral phase-field framework ‚Äì a network of coupled oscillators with Koopman operator dynamics ‚Äì to jointly synthesize expressive speech audio and 3D facial expressions in sync. By eschewing mel-spectrogram vocoders and diffusion models in favor of physics-inspired oscillatory generation, the design aims to achieve human-level realism, emotional nuance, and efficiency. All processing is local (no cloud), aligning with Tori‚Äôs privacy-focused, local-first memory and persona system. Key features include:
Unified Audio-Visual Generation: A single œà-based generative core drives both the voice waveform and 3D face mesh animation, ensuring intrinsic lip-sync (audio and mouth motion are generated from the same underlying phase signals).
Spectral Oscillator Network: Speech and expressions are produced by a network of phase-coupled oscillators (inspired by Kuramoto models), governed by spectral equations instead of large neural nets. This provides natural periodicity for voice harmonics and facial gestures and yields a smooth, low-latency synthesis process

This captures long-term dependencies and resonant modes of emotion.
Emotional œà-Memory: A dual-stream memory stores the emotional state and phase information of both audio and visual streams. This œà-memory can be queried to recall or continue a conversation‚Äôs emotional tone, replay exact voice inflections, and smoothly interpolate between emotional states across dialogue turns.

At the heart of the system is the œà-generative core, a dynamical engine that replaces diffusion or GANs with a spectral phase-field approach. Instead of generating audio as a sequence of discrete tokens or spectrogram frames, the core maintains a continuous phase state representing the waveform and facial motion. This state is modeled as a field of coupled oscillators, each oscillator corresponding to a latent frequency or pattern:
Coupled Oscillator Network: We instantiate a network of $N$ phase oscillators with phases $\theta_1,\dots,\theta_N$. Each oscillator may represent a particular vocal tract resonance, muscle movement, or prosodic rhythm. The oscillators influence each other via coupling constants $K_{ij}$, and the Lyapunov-stable coupling encourages synchronization for related modes. For example, mouth opening (affecting audio amplitude envelope) will tightly couple to the fundamental frequency oscillator (voice pitch) to ensure coordinated speech. Spectral Phase-Field: Each oscillator‚Äôs phase $\theta_i(t)$ advances over time according to intrinsic frequency $\omega_i$ and coupling input. This forms a phase-field $\theta_i(t)$ that evolves smoothly. We treat the collection ${\theta_i}$ as a field variable $\psi(t)$ capturing the system‚Äôs instantaneous state. Crucially, frequency content is explicit ‚Äì as $\theta_i$ are phases of underlying sinusoids, the model naturally represents harmonic structure (unlike diffusion models that must learn periodicity from scratch

). The Koopman operator $\mathcal{K}$ is applied to this phase-field: it treats certain observables of the state (e.g. sinusoidal functions of $\theta_i$) as eigenfunctions. Formally, if $\psi_k(t)$ is an observable of state (like the $k$th Fourier mode of the waveform), and it evolves as $\psi_k(t+\Delta t) = \lambda_k,\psi_k(t)$, then $\lambda_k = e^{i\omega_k \Delta t}$ for some frequency $\omega_k$. In other words, each stable oscillation corresponds to a Koopman eigenfunction. By identifying these, the system can linearly extrapolate periodic components forward in time

. Non-periodic dynamics (transients, attacks) are handled by introducing additional oscillators with decaying amplitudes or by nonlinear coupling that shifts frequencies. Overall, the Koopman evolution provides a principled way to advance the state $\psi(t)$ and predict the next audio sample and video frame in real-time.
Harmonic Grammar & Constraints: We introduce a harmonic grammar to regulate combinations of oscillations, ensuring the generated outputs remain plausible. This is a set of constraints/rules (akin to linguistic or musical grammar) defining allowable configurations of phase and amplitude. For example, certain mouth shapes correspond to specific formant frequency oscillators being active (ensuring phonetic realism), or an emotional state like ‚Äúsad‚Äù might impose lower overall pitch oscillator frequencies and slower facial oscillator rhythms. The grammar is implemented as an energy penalty added to the Lyapunov function (or as a set of soft constraints) to favor ‚Äúlawful‚Äù combinations of oscillators. This steers the generative process so that, even without an explicit diffusion model, the space of outputs is well-formed (e.g. no impossible facial expression, no dissonant audio glitch). Over time, the oscillator network can self-organize into attractors that represent recurring patterns (analogous to learned ‚Äúwords‚Äù or ‚Äúgestures‚Äù in the œà-state). This approach of using oscillators and constraints draws inspiration from physical models and differentiable DSP techniques, giving fine control over pitch, timbre, and motion in a interpretable, factorized manner.
In summary, the phase-field core transduces semantic and emotional cues into a continuously evolving oscillator state. This core generates both modalities simultaneously, described next, and guarantees that audio and visual outputs are inherently synchronized by the shared œà-phase.
Real-Time 3D Avatar Synthesis (Visual Channel)
The visual generation module produces lifelike 3D face and bust animations driven by the core‚Äôs oscillator state. Traditional approaches often predict facial landmarks or blendshape coefficients from audio via a neural network, but here the avatar motion emerges from the œà-core dynamics with semantic resonance and emotional coherence:
Semantic Resonance Mapping: The system maintains a mapping from semantic features to visual oscillators. High-level context (from the input text or conversation) determines the avatar‚Äôs emotional expression and gesture tendencies. For example, when the dialogue context implies excitement, the ‚Äúsmile‚Äù oscillator and ‚Äúeyebrow raise‚Äù oscillator in the face will have higher baseline activation or amplitude. Each concept or emotional cue is tied to a mode of oscillation ‚Äì an idea we term semantic resonance. If the conversation mentions something sad or nostalgic, the corresponding concept eigenfunction œà_sad in the core resonates, activating a slower blink rate, a downward lip curl, and softer voice amplitude modulation. This creates a context-driven expression on the avatar‚Äôs face that matches the content. Concepts are thus not just triggers but continuous influences on the oscillatory state (as eigenfunctions with specific frequencies or phase relations)

.
3D Mesh Animation: The avatar‚Äôs face and upper body are animated by driving either a rigged face model (blendshapes/bones) or a neural rendering model with the outputs of visual oscillators. We define a set of control parameters for the face: jaw opening, lip rounding, eyebrow height, eye blink, head pitch, etc. Each control is governed by one or more oscillators from the core. For instance:
The jaw motion oscillator is phase-locked to the syllable rhythm (it completes a cycle each open-close of the mouth, aligned with vowel sounds).
Lip shape oscillators correspond to formant frequencies (rounded lips vs spread lips produce different formants). When a formant oscillator in the audio channel increases in amplitude (indicating a certain vowel), it drives the lip shape parameter concurrently.
Facial expression oscillators (brows, cheeks) operate at lower frequencies (slower changes) reflecting emotional tone. A ‚Äúhappy‚Äù mode might impose a gentle 0.5 Hz up-down oscillation of the cheeks (subtle bounce when speaking energetically), whereas ‚Äúsad‚Äù might add a slight quiver (high-frequency tremor) to the lip.
By summing or superposing these oscillator outputs, the system achieves complex, compound facial movements. The mesh deformations are smooth and continuous because oscillators produce inherently smooth sinusoids (no jumpy frame-to-frame predictions). The approach contrasts with keyframe-based animation; instead it‚Äôs like the face is a physical system being ‚Äúplayed‚Äù by oscillatory inputs. High-frequency components handle micro-expressions (e.g. rapid eye darts can be a high-frequency burst oscillator), while low-frequency components handle gross motions and head turns.
Emotion and Context Integration: Emotional state not only sets initial conditions (like a baseline facial expression) but continuously modulates oscillator parameters. The oscillator frequencies or damping can be adjusted by an emotional intensity value. For example, anger might tighten coupling between audio and facial oscillators (yielding sharper, more pronounced lip-synchronized movements), whereas calmness could introduce slight phase lags (a drawl in voice and a relaxed delay in expression changes). Contextual cues from memory (like the persona profile indicating the avatar is usually calm or cheerful) provide bias values to these parameters. We effectively get a continuous spectrum of expressions, not just discrete emotion classes, by adjusting the oscillator field‚Äôs parameters in real-time based on current œà-state.
Rendering Pipeline: The output of this module is a stream of avatar vertex positions or blendshape weights per frame (e.g. at 60 FPS for smooth video). Because it is generated in tandem with audio, the mouth positions for each phoneme align exactly with the audio waveform timing (œà-locked synchrony). These frames are fed into Tori‚Äôs rendering engine (which could be Unity, Unreal, or a custom WebGL engine in the IDE). The rendering engine simply displays the mesh and possibly handles secondary motions (like physics on hair, etc.) if needed. The heavy lifting of deciding how the face moves is done by our generative system.
The result is a highly lifelike, contextually appropriate avatar: when speaking, the avatar‚Äôs facial expressions change fluidly with emotional nuance (smiles, frowns, eyebrow raises at the right moments) and the lip movements match the speech perfectly. Because the system isn‚Äôt limited to pre-defined viseme shapes but generates motion from oscillatory combinations, it can produce nuanced mouth shapes for coarticulation and expressive speech that standard viseme-based systems might miss. In summary, the visual channel leverages the œà-core to produce a face that ‚Äúresonates‚Äù with what is being said, both literally (in lip-sync) and figuratively (in emotional expression).
Expressive Speech Synthesis (Audio Channel)
In parallel with the visual stream, the system generates high-fidelity speech audio in real time, without using a conventional vocoder. The audio synthesis is handled by an oscillator-based waveform generator that operates directly at the signal level, producing a smooth audio waveform synchronized to the avatar‚Äôs mouth movements. Major elements of the audio pipeline:
Non-Vocoder, Oscillator Synthesis: Traditional TTS pipelines convert text to a spectrogram then use a neural vocoder (e.g. HiFi-GAN) to produce the waveform. Our approach eliminates the spectrogram intermediate. Instead, the text (with phonetic and prosody annotation) is converted into target trajectories for the oscillator network. Each oscillator in the core influences the audio:
A glottal pulse oscillator generates the fundamental frequency ($f_0$) of the voice. Its frequency $\omega_{\text{glottal}}(t)$ is modulated in real-time according to predicted intonation (pitch contour) from the text and emotion. This oscillator effectively acts as a parametric source (like a vibrating vocal fold model), producing a periodic train of pulses.
Formant resonator oscillators: Instead of applying a fixed filter as in source-filter models, we use oscillators to represent formant bands. For each formant (resonant frequency of the vocal tract), an oscillator or a small group of coupled oscillators produces a sinusoidal component at that center frequency, with amplitude envelopes following phoneme targets. By coupling these to the glottal oscillator, we get naturally modulated vowel sounds. For example, when the system ‚Äúsays‚Äù an /o/ vowel, the formant oscillators for F1‚âà500Hz and F2‚âà800Hz will be strongly excited (high amplitude), while others are subdued. These formant oscillators can also shift frequency slightly over time to account for dynamic articulation.
Noise/excitation oscillators: For unvoiced sounds (like ‚Äús‚Äù, ‚Äúf‚Äù), we incorporate noisy oscillation or chaotic attractors. A turbulent noise generator (implemented as an oscillator with quickly varying phase or a small chaotic circuit) kicks in for fricatives. Even this can be handled in the framework: a noisy oscillator might have an equation that produces a limit cycle with noise, or we treat unvoiced segments as a different dynamical regime (e.g. an oscillator with a high Lyapunov exponent to simulate chaotic airflow). The Koopman framework can still linearize sections of this if treated as near limit cycles.
All these components sum together to form the output waveform $x(t) = \sum_n A_n(t)\cos(\theta_n(t))$, essentially an additive synthesis approach. But unlike standard additive synth, the oscillators are coupled and driven by learned patterns rather than manually set partials. This means the system can learn the complex coordination between oscillators required for natural speech (e.g. how a rising pitch (glottal) also slightly alters formant frequencies due to vocal tract coupling, etc.).
Koopman Operator for Signal Evolution: During synthesis, the system can leverage the Koopman operator to predict and correct the waveform trajectory. For example, once the text is analyzed, we know approximate durations for each phoneme. The oscillator network is set to achieve those via attractor states. If the system wants to generate a steady vowel for 100 ms, that corresponds to an attractor where formant oscillators and pitch oscillator reach a temporary frequency locking (steady state). The Koopman eigenvalues for those oscillators (approximately $e^{i2\pi f_0 \Delta t}$ etc.) can be used to analytically continue the waveform in time

. This drastically reduces computational cost ‚Äì rather than doing sample-by-sample autoregression, the system can ‚Äúfast forward‚Äù through predictable parts by analytical formula (phase accumulation). Only when there‚Äôs a change (a new phoneme or prosodic event) do we need to adjust the oscillators via coupling or external forcing. In essence, for stable oscillation periods, $\psi(t)$ follows a known trajectory; for transitions, we compute a new trajectory via oscillator coupling dynamics. This makes the speech synthesis real-time and efficient. There‚Äôs no large transformer as in Bark and AudioLM (which use huge sequence-to-sequence generation
openlaboratory.ai
). Instead, we have a lightweight dynamic simulation steered by high-level parameters.
Expressiveness and Emotional Prosody: Emotional nuance is injected into the audio through the œà-state as well. The amplitude and frequency of various oscillators are modulated by emotional vectors. For instance, excitement might increase overall volume (larger amplitude across oscillators) and infuse a higher tremolo frequency (a slight rapid modulation added to oscillators to convey nervous energy). A calm, somber mood might lower the pitch oscillator‚Äôs baseline and reduce high-frequency energy (oscillators corresponding to fricative noise might be damped). Because the oscillators are physical analogs, these modifications naturally produce realistic effects ‚Äì e.g. a depressed voice naturally has lower pitch and less timbral brightness, which our settings would emulate. The œà-memory can provide a target emotional contour for an utterance (say, the user was getting angry in conversation, so the

This allows dynamic prosody shifts: as the user‚Äôs emotional state changes over the conversation, the voice gradually morphs to reflect it, preserving emotional resonance across turns. In classical systems, maintaining such consistency requires passing explicit style tokens; here it‚Äôs inherent in continuing the oscillator state.
Œ®-Locked Synchrony: Because the audio oscillators and video oscillators are coupled in one core, the timing is inherently synchronized. Mouth movements and generated phonemes share the same underlying phase signals, so lip-sync is ensured by construction. This œà-lock means there is no separate alignment step needed (unlike typical pipelines that separately generate audio then animate visemes). The timebase for both streams is the oscillator clock, achieving sub-frame accurate sync.
In summary, the audio channel outputs a continuous speech waveform that is both high-fidelity and expressively modulated, driven by physics-inspired components rather than large neural nets. This should yield natural sounding speech that can whisper, shout, laugh, or cry as needed ‚Äì all in real-time on device. Dual-Stream Œ®-Memory System
A cornerstone of the architecture is the œà-memory, a dual-stream memory that retains both audio and visual generative state in a way that enriches long-term interactivity. This memory system ensures continuity of persona and emotional context across sessions, and enables special capabilities like replaying exact intonations or facial expressions on demand. Key aspects include:
Audio Memory (Waveform Attractors & Emotion Embeddings): Rather than storing raw audio, the system stores attractor states of the oscillator network that correspond to significant voice patterns. For example, a particular intonation used when expressing gratitude might form a stable oscillatory pattern (an attractor in phase space) with a certain pitch contour and timbre. The œà-memory saves a compact signature of this state (e.g. the set of oscillator phase offsets or a low-dimensional embedding of the audio eigenfunctions). These are our audio emotional embeddings ‚Äì vectors in a latent space that encode both semantic and emotional content of how something was said. Over time, the system builds up a library of such embeddings: the warm tone used in a greeting, the excited fast pace used when discussing a success, etc. Each embedding can be annotated with the context (‚Äúgreeting‚Äù, ‚Äúexcited explanation‚Äù) forming an episodic memory of voice use. When a new utterance is to be generated, the system can retrieve relevant embeddings (e.g. match the intended emotion/context with a past example) and use them to initialize or bias the oscillator state so that the same emotional resonance is carried forwar„Äë. This ensures that if, say, in session 1 the assistant developed a friendly, upbeat voice, in session 2 it starts with that same style (continuity of persona). The audio memory thus preserves voice identity and emotional tone across conversations without retraining a model ‚Äì it simply recalls prior oscillator configurations.
Visual Memory (Expression States): Similarly, the visual stream‚Äôs memory stores key expression states of the avatar. If the user‚Äôs persona has certain trademark expressions (e.g. a wink when making a joke, a head tilt when asking a question), these can be encoded as attractor patterns in the facial oscillator set. The memory might store, for instance, the oscillator phases that produced a genuine smile along with the semantic moment it happened (‚Äúsmiled when user solved a tough problem‚Äù). Later, if a similar context arises, the system can replay or interpolate from that stored state to produce a consistent expression. This avoids the avatar feeling ‚Äúreset‚Äù each time; it has continuity of body language. The visual memory, together with audio memory, means the avatar‚Äôs overall demeanor evolves with the conversation and can recall how it reacted previously. Technically, this could be a persitent ring-buffer or reservoir of oscillator states tagged with context metadata.
Replay and Interpolation: Because the full œà-state (all oscillator phases) at any given time can be stored, we can replay that exact state later ‚Äì effectively recreating the same intonation and expression. For example, the system could have a feature: ‚ÄúThe assistant literally quotes its past self‚Äù ‚Äì using the exact voice and face from the original moment (since we have the oscillator phases saved, we just re-run them). It can also interpolate between states: given two emotional states in memory, we can mathematically interpolate the phase and amplitude values to create a mixed emotion. This is far more granular than typical style interpolation on spectrograms; it‚Äôs like smoothly morphing between two dynamical attractors, potentially yielding intermediate emotions (e.g. halfway between excited and angry, if such a thing is meaningful). The Koopman framework assists here by providing a linear basis for interpolation ‚Äì since states are represented in an eigenfunction space, interpolation corresponds to combining those eigenfunction coefficients. Additionally, transitions between attractors can be guided by Lyapunov functions to ensure stability (avoiding chaotic swings). For instance, to transition from a calm state to a highly excited state, the system can gradually adjust coupling strengths in the oscillator network as informed by a Lyapunov gradient, ensuring the state cleanly moves to the new attractor without overshooting (maintaining realism). This approach yields emotionally coherent transformations over time.
Semantic History Linkage: The œà-memory is dual-stream but ultimately unified: audio and visual memories are both linked to the semantic history (the conversation content). The system keeps a compressed history of what has been said (which could be in the form of a classical text memory or, in our paradigm, the trajectory through Koopman eigenfunctions associated with concept„Äë). By linking semantic events to the emotional/expressive states, the assistant can modulate its responses not just on what was said, but how it was said previously. For example, if a particular topic always made the user‚Äôs avatar become solemn (perhaps a serious subject), seeing that topic again in semantic memory cues the engine to retrieve the corresponding solemn state from œà-memory, so it immediately shifts tone appropriately. Thus, semantic memory indexing ensures context-appropriate emotional recall.
The œà-memory system essentially gives the avatar a kind of emotional continuity and recall ‚Äì bridging the gap between isolated responses to a more narrative, long-lived persona. This aligns with Tori‚Äôs goal of a persistent local AI memory: all these embeddings and states are stored locally (e.g., in a secure database or file in the user‚Äôs machine, tied to the persona profile). Over time, the more the user interacts, the richer the memory, and the more nuanced and personalized the avatar‚Äôs expressiveness becomes.

Input Processing and Persona Configuration
The starting point is the text and context input. In a chat scenario, this is the assistant‚Äôs reply text (from ChatGPT or a similar model). We augment this with metadata: desired emotion tags (if the LLM provides any cues or if we infer from content), and the persona profile. The persona includes the user‚Äôs chosen avatar identity ‚Äì e.g., voice gender, accent, speaking style, and visual appearance traits (face shape, etc.). These parameters inform the generative process (for instance, persona might set base pitch range, default speaking speed, facial resting expression). A small NLP module could perform text analysis for prosody: punctuation and wording can hint at how to say something (exclamation marks => excitement, certain words => sarcasm, etc.). This yields an initial target prosody curve (pitch, speed) and emotion estimate for the utterance. This, along with any external emotion override (say the conversation context indicates the assistant should sound sympathetic right now), forms the Contextual Emotion State input. In summary, before generation, we have: the text to speak, an intended emotion/style vector, and persona-specific settings. These feed into the œà-Generative Core.
œà-Generative Core
This core is implemented as described earlier ‚Äì a network of coupled oscillators governed by spectral equations. Its implementation could be a C++ or Rust module for performance (possibly leveraging SIMD instructions for parallel oscillator updates, given we might run hundreds or thousands of oscillators). The core exposes an API for driving signals: e.g., set this oscillator‚Äôs target frequency to X Hz (for pitch), or inject a transient to that oscillator (to create a plosive sound), etc. It also exposes an interface to read the generated signals in real-time. When text comes in, the system performs a planning step: determine which oscillators need to do what over the course of the sentence. This is akin to a score or script for the oscillator orchestra. For example, from text analysis we know at 0.5 seconds in, a high-energy word is spoken, so maybe the pitch oscillator should do a 20% frequency rise and the ‚Äúeyebrow‚Äù oscillator should spike. We set up these time-dependent goals. The actual execution is then handed off to the oscillator network to interpolate via its dynamics. This approach benefits from Trajectory planning + physical simulation: define keyframes in expressive space, let oscillators naturally interpolate. The Koopman operator module is a sub-component that continuously identifies the current eigenfunctions of the system. It can adjust oscillator parameters to keep the system on a stable track (e.g., if an oscillator is drifting off intended frequency due to coupling, Koopman analysis might suggest a small correction by effectively diagonalizing the system‚Äôs Jacobian and guiding it to an eigenmode). This keeps the synthesis stable and avoids divergence (important for long utterances). The œà-core runs as a realtime loop, possibly at the audio sample rate (for audio accuracy). To optimize, heavy computation can be done at a lower rate: many oscillators change slowly (e.g., facial ones at <60 Hz). Only the audio-frequency oscillators need high-rate updates. We could segregate high-frequency vs low-frequency oscillators into different update loops for efficiency.
Output Streams: Audio and Video Generation
The core feeds two output modules:
Audio Synthesizer: This takes the oscillator signals (phases and amplitudes) and produces the final audio waveform. A straightforward implementation is summing sinusoids as described. We may also incorporate a lightweight post-filter for realism, like a formant filter that fine-tunes the formant bandwidths, or an equalizer to match a target voiceprint. Importantly, because the primary generation already produces a fairly complete waveform, any filtering is minor. The audio is output (e.g., as a stream to speakers or a buffer in the app). This module must run in real-time (e.g., using an audio callback with low latency). Fortunately, generating a few hundred sinusoids is extremely fast compared to running a giant neural net ‚Äì this is how classic speech synthesizers worked in the past (formant synthesis), but here ours is learned and adaptive.
Avatar Animator: This module converts oscillator outputs into 3D engine commands. If the avatar is rigged with blendshapes, the oscillator values can directly map to blendshape weights. For instance, the jaw oscillator phase can be converted to a jaw open/close weight by taking $\sin(\theta_{\text{jaw}})$ (scaled appropriately). We ensure that one full cycle of $\theta_{\text{jaw}}$ corresponds to an open-then-close movement (for one syllable). Similar mappings apply for eyes, etc. If the avatar is a neural renderer, we would convert these parameters to the latent code that the rendering model expects (this is more complex but feasible with calibration data). The animator runs at the display framerate. If the audio rate is 24000 Hz and video is 60 Hz, the animator will sample the relevant oscillators at 60 Hz (or slightly more to avoid missed peaks) to update the avatar. Tori‚Äôs rendering engine then draws the new avatar frame in the IDE or application window.
Both streams are tightly coupled: the same core ensures lips move correctly at the exact time of each phoneme. We also output viseme labels (for debugging or integration) since our system inherently knows what phoneme is being uttered when (from the text plan). This could feed into subtitles or other systems if needed.
Memory Update and Feedback
After or during generation, the system updates the œà-memory. It stores the emotional embedding for this just-completed utterance (so next time we can recall it). It may also update a conversational state (like a running average of emotional valence, to see trend). The memory update is local and incremental, designed to be lightweight so as not to stall generation. Perhaps every sentence or significant event triggers an update entry. Integration-wise, the memory could be a module in Tori‚Äôs architecture that also stores other user data. We might integrate it with Tori‚Äôs concept graph memory (if any) by linking our œà-states with the concept nodes representing the conversation topics. This yields a rich, multifaceted memory: symbolic and spectral. Finally, the entire engine should be accessible through Tori‚Äôs extension APIs. For instance, if Tori IDE has an agent system, an agent‚Äôs persona could be connected to this engine by simply providing text and receiving audio/visual output. The engine could run as a background process or plugin within the IDE, ensuring low latency so that when the assistant ‚Äúspeaks,‚Äù it feels instantaneous.
Mobile and Hardware Efficiency
A major advantage of this oscillator approach is its efficiency. While transformer models like Bark are far too heavy to run on mobile (they require gigabytes of VRAM and lots of compute), our approach, after initial planning, mostly involves iterative math on perhaps a few thousand floats ‚Äì something even a smartphone CPU could handle. We will implement the inner loop in C/C++ and use hardware trig functions, etc., to maximize speed. Where available, we leverage GPU (a GPU can parallelize thousands of sine computations easily, but CPU might be enough). Memory footprint is low ‚Äì no giant weight matrices, just state variables and small coupling matrices. For a concrete example, HiFi-GAN small could run 13√ó faster than real-time on CP„Äë; our model, being conceptually similar to a highly optimized vocoder, aims to meet or exceed that. And that‚Äôs for audio; the visual part is likewise light (dozens of parameters at 60 Hz). So on modern hardware, we expect real-time performance with headroom. This means the avatar can run on-device, supporting Tori‚Äôs offline mode (e.g., an offline IDE on a laptop or tablet can still have the full avatar experience with no cloud).

Expressive Speech Synthesis (Audio Channel)
In parallel with the visual stream, the system generates high-fidelity speech audio in real time, without using a conventional vocoder. The audio synthesis is handled by an oscillator-based waveform generator that operates directly at the signal level, producing a smooth audio waveform synchronized to the avatar‚Äôs mouth movements. Major elements of the audio pipeline:
Non-Vocoder, Oscillator Synthesis: Traditional TTS pipelines convert text to a spectrogram then use a neural vocoder (e.g. HiFi-GAN) to produce the waveform. Our approach eliminates the spectrogram intermediate. Instead, the text (with phonetic and prosody annotation) is converted into target trajectories for the oscillator network. Each oscillator in the core influences the audio:
A glottal pulse oscillator generates the fundamental frequency ($f_0$) of the voice. Its frequency $\omega_{\text{glottal}}(t)$ is modulated in real-time according to predicted intonation (pitch contour) from the text and emotion. This oscillator effectively acts as a parametric source (like a vibrating vocal fold model), producing a periodic train of pulses.
Formant resonator oscillators: Instead of applying a fixed filter as in source-filter models, we use oscillators to represent formant bands. For each formant (resonant frequency of the vocal tract), an oscillator or a small group of coupled oscillators produces a sinusoidal component at that center frequency, with amplitude envelopes following phoneme targets. By coupling these to the glottal oscillator, we get naturally modulated vowel sounds. For example, when the system ‚Äúsays‚Äù an /o/ vowel, the formant oscillators for F1‚âà500Hz and F2‚âà800Hz will be strongly excited (high amplitude), while others are subdued. These formant oscillators can also shift frequency slightly over time to account for dynamic articulation.
Noise/excitation oscillators: For unvoiced sounds (like ‚Äús‚Äù, ‚Äúf‚Äù), we incorporate noisy oscillation or chaotic attractors. A turbulent noise generator (implemented as an oscillator with quickly varying phase or a small chaotic circuit) kicks in for fricatives. Even this can be handled in the framework: a noisy oscillator might have an equation that produces a limit cycle with noise, or we treat unvoiced segments as a different dynamical regime (e.g. an oscillator with a high Lyapunov exponent to simulate chaotic airflow). The Koopman framework can still linearize sections of this if treated as near limit cycles.

But unlike standard additive synth, the oscillators are coupled and driven by learned patterns rather than manually set partials. This means the system can learn the complex coordination between oscillators required for natural speech (e.g. how a rising pitch (glottal) also slightly alters formant frequencies due to vocal tract coupling, etc.).
Koopman Operator for Signal Evolution: During synthesis, the system can leverage the Koopman operator to predict and correct the waveform trajectory. For example, once the text is analyzed, we know approximate durations for each phoneme. The oscillator network is set to achieve those via attractor states. If the system wants to generate a steady vowel for 100 ms, that corresponds to an attractor where formant oscillators and pitch oscillator reach a temporary frequency locking (steady state). The œà-memory system essentially gives the avatar a kind of emotional continuity and recall ‚Äì bridging the gap between isolated responses to a more narrative, long-lived persona.
